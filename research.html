Research Narratives

"From noisy observations to structural truths: My journey through statistical discovery."

I. The Architecture of Growth: Discovering Dynamical Laws

Current Doctoral Research | UMBC

The fundamental sciences are often governed by unseen laws of growth, yet in the real world, these laws are masked by the persistent hum of Gaussian measurement error. My current work seeks to peel back this noise. By treating the evolutionary state as a dynamical system, I have developed a methodology rooted in Locally Weighted Polynomial Regression (LPR).

The heart of this discovery lies in a delicate balance: minimizing two distinct loss functions—one that captures the local smoothing of the trajectory and another that enforces the linear dynamics of the system. Whether through sequential refinement or simultaneous shrinkage, this approach allows for the recovery of underlying differential relationships without the constraints of global parametric assumptions. By extending this to multi-subject scenarios through random effects, I am building tools that respect individual heterogeneity while uncovering the universal equations that drive longitudinal systems.

II. Navigating the Evolutionary Tree

Collaborative Research with Dr. Dootika Vats (2023)

Biological evolution leaves behind a complex, high-dimensional trail. During my time at IIT Kanpur, I immersed myself in the world of Bayesian Phylogenetic Inference, where the goal was to reconstruct the history of life through Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. This was a journey of algorithmic engineering—creating new tree topologies and testing the sensitivity of likelihood functions to ensure we weren't just sampling data, but uncovering robust posterior probabilities. It was a project defined by the challenge of exploring vast, discrete parameter spaces to find the most probable lineages of our past.

III. The Search for Finite Variance in Prediction

Collaborative Research with Dr. Dootika Vats (2022)

In high-dimensional Bayesian modeling, the ability to predict the unknown is everything. However, estimating that accuracy often leads to high-variance pitfalls. I focused on implementing a novel mixture estimator for Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOO-CV). This work was a bridge between simplicity and mathematical rigor—demonstrating how a mixture approach could maintain the ease of classical methods while providing the crucial stability of finite variance. By deriving the variance of the Expected Log Predictive Density via batch means, I helped establish a more reliable yardstick for model performance in complex predictive landscapes.
